Phishing attacks account for 95% of all successful cyber attacks worldwide, and these attacks can produce disastrous consequences for individual users, in the form of identity theft, to Fortune-100 Companies, in the way of data breaches. Research has shown that despite existing security indicators in the browser and email software, users of all technology levels still fall for these attacks, as phishing essentially manipulates human behavior. Because interfaces do not present website URLs in a way that captures attention in time, people accidentally click on such links or fail to notice warning signs and become victims of phishing attacks.


Through my Honors Thesis, I am researching and designing proactive changes to the email interface that will capture a user’s attention and prevent them from clicking on malicious links. I am using principles from visual and cognitive psychology research as the basis for creating these components. A lot of previous research base their findings on observational studies that test a user’s awareness of security indicators and lead to suggestions for design improvements. Psychology lends overall deeper insight into how humans interact and perceive information on an interface. This more in-depth insight, paired with existing research, can be used to find solutions that are in line with a user’s typical behavior.


I am currently in my second semester of research, and I am designing prototypes with my suggested design changes. I will create an experiment later this semester that sees users interacting with regular emails and my redesigned emails, and see how effective my changes are in preventing phishing.
